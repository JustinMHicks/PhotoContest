  • The Cloisters
    •

    Perhaps nowhere in New York quite captures the sheer opulence of its 19th century robber barons as the Cloisters. This opulence is manifested not by what is there, but rather from what isn’t. Look across the Hudson and you see the great forested cliffs of the New Jersey Palisades. These cliffs are devoid of development because J.P. Morgan and J.D. Rockefeller bought up the many aristocratic estates that lined this part of the river and planted forests to give the view from the Cloisters, the Met’s medieval wing, a convincingly Middle Ages air. The building itself was assembled by Rockefeller money in the 20’s and 30’s. The family paid for the relocation and reassembly of five different Spanish and French medieval buildings. If you look at the building itself, you can tell that it’s really just a bunch of different architectural elements put together.

  • Riverbank State Park
    •

    Riverbank is the only park of its kind in the Western Hemisphere. Inspired by urban rooftop designs in Japan, this 28-acre multi-level landscaped recreational facility is a state-of-the-art park facility. Rising 69 feet above the Hudson River, Riverbank offers a wide variety of recreational, athletic and arts experiences for all ages, interests and abilities.Housed in five major buildings are an Olympic-size pool, a covered skating rink for roller skating in the summer and ice-skating in the winter, an 800-seat cultural theater, a 2,500-seat athletic complex with fitness room, and a 150-seat restaurant.Outdoor sports amenities include a 25-yard lap pool, a wading pool, four tennis courts, four basketball courts, a softball field, four hand/paddleball courts, and a 400-meter eight-lane running track with a football/soccer field.Riverbank also boasts spectacular promenade views of the Hudson River, the Palisades and the George Washington Bridge. At water level, there is a 400-seat amphitheater and docking facilities for excursion and fishing boats.

  • Duarte Square (CU Med and NYP Hospital)
    •

    In the late 1600s the land that is now Juan Pablo Duarte Square was developed as a farm by Trinity Church. A forty-foot-wide canal was built to the south in 1810 to drain the pestilent Collect Pond into the Hudson River. The canal was filled in 1819 and now forms Canal Street. As the city spread northward, this became an important commercial thoroughfare. Canal Street achieved further prominence with the construction of the Holland Tunnel at its western end in 1927.Juan Pablo Duarte Square was officially dedicated in 1945, when Sixth Avenue was renamed Avenue of the Americas in celebration of Pan-American unity. The name of the square, located near the southern end of the Avenue of the Americas, honors Juan Pablo Duarte (1813-1876), the liberator of the Dominican Republic. As a young man, Duarte founded a society called La Trinitaria which sought to promote democratic ideals among the Spanish-speaking inhabitants of Hispaniola Island, most of whom were clustered around the city of Santo Domingo. In 1843 Duarte launched an attempt to free the eastern half of the island from Haitian rule. When the rebellion failed, Duarte fled Hispaniola. However, when a new revolution succeeded in winning independence for the Dominican Republic in February 1844, Duarte was invited to return as President of the new republic. Although he eventually lost control to a military dictator and died in exile, Duarte was instrumental in developing the Pan-American traditions of democracy and self-government celebrated by the Avenue of the Americas.

    Duarte Square, a triangular plot bounded by Sullivan Street, Grand Street, and the Avenue of the Americas at the intersection with Canal Street, was initially developed and maintained by the Department of Transportation. The square was improved in 1975 with the addition of benches, trees, and sidewalks.On May 26, 1977, Duarte Square was transferred to the Department of Parks. A statue of Juan Pablo Duarte, donated by the Consulate of the Dominican Republic, was dedicated in the square on the 165th anniversary of Duarte’s birth, January 26, 1978. The thirteen-foot bronze figure, which rests atop an eight-foot granite base, was designed by the Italian sculptor Nicola Arrighini. It is one of a pantheon of six monuments to Latin American leaders which overlook the Avenue of the Americas.

  • Harriet Tubman Memorial Plaza
    •

    This larger-than-life bronze sculpture depicts abolitionist organizer and Underground Railroad leader Harriet Tubman (c. 1822-1913), and stands at the crossroads of St. Nicholas Avenue, West 122nd Street and Frederick Douglass Boulevard in Harlem. Douglass once said of Tubman that except for John Brown, he knew of "no one who has willingly encountered more perils and hardships to serve our enslaved people."Born into slavery in Maryland around 1822, Tubman escaped in 1849 via the Underground Railroad, the network of places and people dedicated to helping slaves find their way to freedom in non-slaveholding communities. Settling first in Philadelphia, then Canada, Tubman spent ten years returning to Maryland at great personal risk, to guide scores of friends and family members to freedom. Determined to end slavery, she later served the Union Army as a scout, spy and nurse in the Civil War. Settling in Auburn, New York after the war, she continued campaigning for equal rights for women and African-Americans. Her humanitarian work, including caring for the sick, homeless and disabled of all races, resulted in the establishment of the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged in that community. She died in 1913 and was buried in Fort Hill Cemetery in Auburn with semi-military honors.

    The memorial, commissioned through the Department of Cultural Affairs' Percent for Art program, was created by African-American sculptor Alison Saar. The artist has depicted Tubman "not as the conductor of the Underground Railroad but as the train itself, an unstoppable locomotive,” the roots of slavery pulled up in her wake. Saar designed stylized portraits of "anonymous passengers" of the Underground Railroad in Tubman's skirt, some of which were inspired by West African "passport masks." Around the granite base of the monument are bronze tiles alternately depicting events in Tubman's life and traditional quilting patterns.The $2.8 million, multi-agency project, which included the landscaping of a formerly barren traffic triangle, was sponsored by former Manhattan Borough President C. Virginia Fields. Designed by Quennell Rothschild and constructed by URS, the renovated triangle features paving blocks and roughly hewn granite to create a natural setting. Plantings native to both New York and Tubman's home state of Maryland represent the woods and terrain traveled by Tubman and her Underground Railroad passengers, providing a contemplative space in which to consider Tubman's legacy.

  • City College Campus
    •

    City College was originally known as the Free Academy of the City of New York. It was founded in 1847 by Townsend Harris, then the president of the Board of Education. The goal for the school was to provide children from low-income homes an opportunity to get a higher education for free. The academy later was renamed The College of the City of New York in 1866. The name of the college continued to change until finally became City College for the University of New York. The school was originally located in downtown Manhattan from 1849 to 1907. The design for the building was created by James Renwick Jr. The building didn’t last for long; it was destroyed in 1928 and was replaced in 1930. The college was then moved to Manhattanville in 1906. The designer of the new neo-gothic campus was George Browne Post. The neo-gothic style is derived from the original gothic architectural style and includes distinctive decorative patterns, finials, scalloping, lancet windows, hood moldings, and label stops. The gothic style of architecture is presented in the Shepard Hall, Baskerville Hall, Compton Hall, Harris Hall, Wingate Hall. Although the college started of being free, it isn’t free anymore. However, there are many scholarship opportunities available.

  • Inwood Hill Park
    •

    The largest village in pre-colonial New York was located on the plain below Inwood Hill. Perhaps several thousand ethnic Algonquins (specifically Wiechquaegecks or “Wappingers”) lived in a village located at the present site of 204th Street and Seaman Avenue. Human burials have been discovered in the area, and the only complete Indian pot ever to excavated in Manhattan was taken out of the ground at 204th and Tenth. The caves in the park were inhabited and used as summer fishing and oystering camps long before the intensive Algonquin settlement of the neighborhood, which occurred as early as the first human settlement of the region 10,000 years ago. Inwood Hill Park is the last untouched forest on the island of Manhattan; watch out for snakes in the rocks!

  • High Bridge Park
    •

    Without a pump to give it pressure, water from an aqueduct will only rise as high as its source’s elevation. In pre-electric New York, this meant no water could rise past the sixth story of most buildings. The solution: water towers. Water pressure for upper floors was provided by water towers such as this one, built in 1872. Now defunct, this tower stands as a memory of one of New York’s boldest engineering projects to date. Water from the Croton Reservoir upstate passed through the High Bridge Aqueduct, the city’s oldest bridge, and traveled down Manhattan to the main reservoir that once stood at Bryant Park.

Upper East Side

  • 68th Street and Park Avenue
    •

    If you want an idea of what Park Avenue, formerly Fourth Avenue, was like before the rail tunnels that run under it were fully enclosed, look no further than Brooklyn’s Atlantic Avenue. Today, the Long Island Rail Road runs in deep cuts or on elevateds for much of the length of of the avenue. The presence of the rail has made the area undesirable for residential development. Atlantic cuts a desolate, industrial path through the residential heart of north Brooklyn. Before Park Avenue was covered and steam engines and heavy rail traffic still made their presence felt, smelt, and heard, the Upper East Side quite separated from the rest of the city. When the tunnel was built, everything changed and Park Avenue became among the wealthiest places on Earth. The high rise at 740 Park Avenue has the most billionaires of any apartment building in the country.

  • Carl Schurz Park
    •

    In the 1790’s, this part of the Upper East Side was a bit like what the Hamptons is today--an exurban beachfront lined with the weekend homes of the wealthy. The Gracie Mansion, the official residence of the mayors of New York since 1942, is the last remaining home of this kind and one of the oldest wooden structures on Manhattan. The park celebrates Carl Schurz, a German revolutionary from the botched German Unification of 1848 who fled to America and later became a U.S. Senator.

  • Mount Sinai Hospital
    •

    Mount Sinai Hospital was founded in 1852 to address the health problems of the rapidly growing Jewish immigrant population in New York City. Originally named "The Jews' Hospital", the founding location was built on 28th Street between 7th and 8th avenue. As medical needs increased for the entire population of New York, the hospital changed it's name to Mount Sinai to reflect it's Jewish heritage, yet become more inviting to members of other religious groups. Mount Sinai Hospital has a long list of accomplishments including being the site of the first liver transplant and pioneered using genetically engineered vaccines to treat the flu. Among the distinguished students and doctors that have passed through these halls are our very own Sameer Kahn and Nadav Nahumi.

  • Weill Cornell Hospital
    •

    Weill Cornell Medical College was founded on April 14, 1898 by Oliver H. Payne. In 1998 the college partnered with Presbyterian Hospital and was renamed The New York Presbyterian Hospital. Despite this merger, the teachings of Weill Cornell are preserved in the New York Presbyterian Hospital. It has 24 basic science and patient care programs available and provides an educational program that focuses on patient-care. Thus, the merger of Weill Cornell and Presbyterian Hospital helped strengthen the academic programs that the schools provide. In 2004 the Weill Cornell medical school opened the first American Medical school outside of United States Border in Qatar.

  • Rockefeller University
    •

    The Rockefeller University was founded in 1901 by John D. Rockefeller Sr. as The Rockefeller Institute for Medical Research. The first institution in the United States devoted solely to using biomedical research to understand the underlying causes of disease, Rockefeller is today one of the foremost biomedical research centers in the world, and its scientists have made numerous seminal contributions to biology and medicine. Over the years, 24 scientists associated with the university have received Nobel Prizes and 21 have earned the Albert Lasker Medical Research Award. Fourteen Rockefeller scientists have received the Gairdner Foundation International Award and 14 have been given theNational Medal of Science, the nation's highest scientific honor. In addition to a century of prize-winning breakthroughs, Rockefeller has devoted over half a century to training the next generation of innovative, pathbreaking scientists.

  • Museum Mile
    •

    Museum Mile is the term given in 1978 to the collection of Museums on 5th Avenue from 105th St all the way down to 82nd Street. The ten-museum collection includes notable landmarks such as El Museo Del Barrio, The Africa Center, The Museum of the City of New York, the Guggenheim, and the Metropolitan Museum of Art. Although the Frick Collection of art is also on 5th Ave., it sits on the corner of 70th Street and is therefore, not part of the Museum Mile. East Side

  • Roosevelt Island
    •

    Roosevelt Island lies between Manhattan and Queens in the middle of the East River. It gets it's name not from Theodore Roosevelt (the war-hero, conservationist President) but instead from Franklin D. Roosevelt (the President who instituted the New Deal following the Great Depression). Make sure to see the Jacob Blackwell House built in 1796 when the island was privately owned. It is one of the oldest surviving structures in New York City. In 1828, Blackwell sold his island to the city of New York and within the next decade, the city opened a penitentiary and lunatic asylum on the land. Soon after, the city would again house undesirable citizens when the Smallpox Hospital was opened in 1856. Due to this reputation the island was named Welfare Island in 1921 (this was obviously before political correctness).

    In the 1970s, the island was renamed Roosevelt Island to signify a shift in the way New Yorkers have perceived the land and it's inhabitants in the last 50 years. A tramway over the East River for the cost of a subway ride (prominently featured in the Spiderman films), high-rise luxury condominiums, a free bus service, and a recently completed park on the southern tip makes the island an attractive place to call home.

  • NYPL Main Branch
    •

    This flagship building for the New York Public Library is also a landmark in Midtown Manhattan. The building is made out of marble that is 3ft thick and is guarded by two iconic stone lions made of Tennessee marble. The two lions, named "Patience" and "Fortitude" were sculpted by a man named Edward Clark Potter. The goal for the library was to have a develop an efficient system that would allow people who want books to receive them as soon as possible. The library was enlarged by 125,000 square feet in the 1980’s due to the growth of the research collection. To this day, the history and architecture of the building has caused it to be featured in many movies and pieces of literature. If you have time, check out the fabled reading room located on one of the upper floors of the library - but be sure to turn off all cell phones and speak quietly so as not to disturb the patrons studying within!

  • Tudor City
    •

    Tudor City was the first residential skyscraper in the world developed by Fred French in 1932. Previously, First Avenue in the Upper East Side was a gang-ridden Irish community and a haven for criminals that earned the name "Corcoran's Roost" from Jimmy Corcoran, founder of the Rag Gang. In an effort to clean up the neighborhood, residents transformed the grounds into a miniature 18-hole golf course, and tennis courts that were flooded and frozen in the winter to be re-purposed as skating rinks. Tudor City gets it's name from the neo-Gothic architecture and embellishments that adorn the building. While you're here, look across First Avenue and check out the United Nations building!

  • Ford Foundation Atrium
    •

    The Ford Foundation building and atrium, completed in 1967, were the product of a collaboration between Dan Kiley and architects Kevin Roche and John Dinkeloo of Kevin Roche John Dinkeloo and Associates. The building’s exterior is largely composed of glass panels, Cor-ten steel and brown-pink granite, with the south and east facades enclosed in glass and twelve stories of office space on the north and west sides, which overlook the atrium. The glass walls create a temperate environment that is ideal for the atrium’s subtropical garden, while also creating a seamless flow of green space between the atrium and Tudor City Park to the east.

    Kiley’s landscape plan, installed on the ground floor of the 160-foot tall atrium, is driven by the thirteen-foot grade change between the 42nd and 43rd Street building entrances. The primary staircase connecting these entrances is broken into three sections, the landings of which access tiered garden terraces at three levels. The terraces step down to the garden’s focal point, a square still-water pool edged with a wide concrete band that sits at the atrium’s lowest level. The terraces were originally planted with a dozen saucer magnolias, low shrubs and groundcovers, which were later replaced with a variety of lush subtropical plants better suited to the interior environment. Planters line the edges of the third, fourth, fifth, and eleventh floors, taking the planting palette into the upper tiers of the atrium. In the mid-1990s the Ford Foundation building was named a New York City Landmark.

  • Grand Central Terminal
    •

    In 1854, New Yorkers banned steam engines from going south of 42nd street, which was then the edge of town. To get further downtown, engines would detach and the cars would be pulled downtown by horses to a terminal at 27th Street and from there to City Hall via Park Avenue, Fourth Avenue, and the Bowery. Through the Civil War period, Cornelius Vanderbilt and his New York Central bought up the three other rail companies that operated on the line, forming a rail empire that would make Vanderbilt among the most powerful men in America. A rail station called Grand Central Depot was first built at 42nd and Park in 1871 and was rebuilt into Grand Central Terminal in 1913. The original design called for a skylight instead of the famous painting of the constellations and foundational supports for an office building atop the station.

    In 1903, McKim, Meade and White, the most famous architectural firm in turn-of-the-century New York, produced this plan for Grand Central Terminal. Note the open rail yards north of the station--Park Avenue wasn’t fully enclosed until the new station was opened in 1913.

  • Grand Army Plaza
    •

    Fronted by one of the most famous hotels in the world, the Plaza Hotel, Manhattan’s Grand Army Plaza (as opposed to Brooklyn’s, which is certainly this plaza’s grander younger brother) marks the southeastern extent of Central Park. The plaza is focused upon Augustus Saint-Gaudens striking gilt (yes, that’s real gold) equestrian statue of Union Civil War hero William Tecumseh Sherman. Saint-Gaudens was a prolific sculptor who dominated the New York art world at the turn of the twentieth century. The designer of several US coins from the period, Saint-Gaudens also designed the gold statue that sits atop the Municipal Building downtown and the golden Diana that once graced the top of the original Madison Square Garden (at Madison Square Park; where else?). The man celebrated, William Tecumseh Sherman, is widely considered to be one of the originators of modern “total war” because of his army’s decimation of the Georgian countryside during the last days of the Civil War. He is reviled as a war criminal in parts of the South.

Upper West Side

  • Boat Basin Cafe
    •

    The 79th Street Boat Basin was constructed in 1937 as part of a project to cover and repurpose the area which once was an access point to the no longer existing New York West Side Railroad. Boaters pay a yearly fee starting at $5,000 to dock their boats at the slip.

  • Museum of Natural History
    •

    The American Natural History Museum is one of the largest and most celebrated museums in the world. Located on the Upper West side of Manhattan, the museum was founded on April 6, 1869. The original building had a gothic design that was designed by J. Wrey Mould in 1877, but since then there have been various renovations to the interior and exterior of the building. With 45 permanent exhibition halls housing over 32 million specimens, this museum can take several days to explore thoroughly! Also, in 1992, an eight-story museum library (designed by the Roche-Dinkeloo firm) opened it's doors nearby. Unlike the New York Public Library, patrons must be on official business with a university or city organization to peruse the important and sometimes ancient documents within.

  • Grant's Tomb
    •

    Once among the most popular tourist attractions in New York City, Grant’s Tomb is the final resting place of Ulysses S. Grant, the 18th President of the United States. Nicknamed “Unconditional Surrender” Grant during the Civil War, Ulysses S. Grant was widely credited with winning the war for the Union side. His scandal-beleaguered administration presided over America’s intensifying industrialization, a railroad building bonanza, and the addition of an amendment to the constitution (the fifteenth) which reads, “The right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude.” Grant’s face graces our $50 note to this day.

  • Hudson River Park
    •

    Hudson River Park, the longest waterfront park in the United States, has transformed four miles of decaying piers and parking lots along Manhattan's West side into a beloved, urban recreational paradise. Attracting 17 million visits each year, the Park offers a myriad of recreational and educational activities for local residents and visitors alike, and plays a critical role in protecting the Hudson River environment itself. In its brief history, Hudson River Park has also served as an important catalyst for economic development and job creation. Over the past decade, it has attracted $3 billion in new construction at 94 new buildings in adjacent neighborhoods. The inspiration for numerous waterfront parks, city- and nation-wide, Hudson River Park represents the next great era in the history of urban parks -- the birth of the waterfront park. Today, people visit Hudson River Park to cycle the busiest bikeway in America, to sunbathe on green beaches, and to connect with the river. More than 135,000 people enjoyed free concerts, movies and dances here last summer. Over 400,000 people of all ages play on the Park's beloved athletic fields and courts. And 7,500 children participate in free educational programming each year. Hudson River Park is deeply loved and appreciated by New Yorkers not only for the magnificent open space it provides, but also because it serves so many needs.

  • The Cathedral of St.John the Divine
    •

    “Cathedral” comes from the Latin word for “chair” and can only apply to a church that is the principal church of a diocese--the seat, or “chair,” of the local bishop. By this definition, St. John the Divine is the largest cathedral in the world and is one of the top five largest church buildings in the world. Still under construction after more than 130 years, the cathedral boasts some of the strangest religious art in the city. One of the most peculiar is a wall of twenty sculptures, each representing the great personages of the twenty Christian centuries. The sculptures representing the twentieth century are full body Medieval style portraits of Martin Luther King, Gandhi, Susan B. Anthony, and Albert Einstein.

  • Riverside Baptist Church
    •

    Located in Morningisde Heights on a high bluff overlooking the Hudson River, Riverside Baptist Church is one of the most icnonic religious structures in New York City. An ecumenical (meaning many faiths) church, with its roots in the Baptist faith, it's history refelcts the modernist religious theology of it's founder pastor Harry Fosidck. Finacned with figts from members of the church (most notable being John D Rockefeller Jr.), the neo-Gothic complex was built between 1928 and 1930. It is comprised of three distinct sections: a five-bay nave and chancel extending north toward West 122nd, a mid-block twenty-two story tower housing the narthex, and a one and a half story clositer passage extending East to Claremont Avenue. The architects based the structure on the design of several French Gothic structures. Built in an era when most Manhattan churches were being overshadowed by secular skyscrapers, the 392-foot tower provides the congregation with a strong presence on the uptown skyline. It's easy to imagine the awe and wonder this towering building inspires and the overtures to the grandness of the diety it was erected in honor of.

West Side

  • Times Square
    •

    Formerly Longacre Square, Times Square was renamed in April 1904 after The New York Times moved its headquarters to the newly erected Times Building – now called One Times Square – and site of the annual ball drop on New Year's Eve, a tradition which began on December 31, 1907 and continues today, attracting thousands to the Square every New Year's. Times Square is the intersection of spectators and performers, tourists and locals. The square accommodates many activities both planned and spontaneous, and connects streetscapes, underground passages, and penthouses. Much of what constitutes modern American culture has been invented and reinvented, tested, and displayed in the few blocks that make up the Times Square district. In 1928, some 264 shows were produced in 76 theaters in Times Square. These theaters showcased not old-world opera, but the new popular culture born of America’s immigrant stew – vaudeville and musicals, jazz and the movies. Prostitution and sex theaters defined the area for much of the post-World War II era. In a larger sense, Times Square was a place where boundaries could be pushed, and broken, and desire expressed. It is no accident that, in Times Square, women could challenge the rules of dating, and gays and lesbians could find a greater level of freedom than they found elsewhere in the city. Times Square blossomed in the first third of the twentieth century, only to slide into notorious decay in the face of the post-1945 world of television, suburbs, and racial strife. Times Square has returned in the past two decades. The crowds that first made the place have also returned, contributing to the unique mix of creativity and commerce, energy and edge that makes Times Square the central attraction of New York City.

  • Bryant Park
    •

    The city acquired the area in 1822 to make the current site of Bryant Park into a graveyard for its unknown and homeless poor. In the 1840’s, this was the southern terminus of the Croton Aqueduct, which ended in a massive reservoir with fifty foot high walls. Adjacent to this was New York’s Crystal Palace, a grand all-glass structure built in 1851. One of the first glass structures ever built, the Crystal Palace was the centerpiece of an international industrial expo that brought over one million visitors to New York in 1853. One in twenty four Americans visited the Crystal Palace Exposition. Imagine today if 13 million people were to converge on this space! A renowned architectural wonder, the Crystal Palace was destroyed in a fire in 1858. The reservoir was replaced by the current library building in 1900.

  • Columbus Circle
    •

    About ten thousand people gathered in Columbus Circle on October 12, 1892, to celebrate the 400th anniversary of the arrival of Christopher Columbus in the New World. Together they dedicated the Columbus Monument, designed by Sicilian sculptor Gaetano Russo and donated to the City of New York by the Italian-American community. The monument consists of a statue of Columbus posed on a column mounted on a base surrounded by fountains and a figure depicting the Genius of Discovery stands on the base. Look for the bronze seals that portray Columbus’s journey, as well as an American bald eagle and lotus-shaped crest.Columbus Circle is remarkable not only for its central monument but also for the subways beneath it and the collection of buildings clustered around it. Workers managed to dig around and beneath the monument’s 1.5 million-ton foundations in order to complete the IRT subway tunnel and Columbus Circle station in 1902.Two recent projects have made Columbus Circle more inviting. The monument was restored in time for the 500th anniversary of Columbus’s journey in 1992 and in 1998 several city agencies including City Planning, Transportation, and Parks collaborated on an interim design for Columbus Circle. The roadways have been reconfigured, pedestrian walkways have been improved, and the public can now enjoy a beautiful and green sitting area in the middle of a major traffic hub.

  • Rockefeller Center
    •

    The famous Rockefeller Center in Midtown Manhattan is an Art Deco NYC landmark. Spanning 22 acres and featuring 19 commercial buildings in New York, Rockefeller Center is one of the most popular attractions in NYC. A popular New York destination all year long, the Rockefeller Center is home to numerous NY events, including the lighting of the famous Rockefeller Center Christmas Tree, which is broadcasted live on TV around the world each year. Built in 1939, Rockefeller Center was the vision of famous NY businessman and philanthropist, John D. Rockefeller. This iconic NYC landmark has been featured in dozens of films and TV shows over the past century, and is home to the legendary NBC Studios and Saturday Night Live. With its serene statues and beautiful art nouveau murals, Rockefeller Center is also a popular destination for art enthusiasts in NYC. You'll notice a lot of figures from Greek mythology in the architecture, including Zeus, Prometheus, Hermes, Atlas, and several muses and maidens.

  • Lincoln Square
    •

    Greenwich Village’s Washington Square was named for President George Washington, and the Flatiron District’s Madison Square was named for President James Madison. Thus, isn’t it safe to assume that the Upper West Side’s Lincoln Square must have been named for President Abraham Lincoln? It turns out there no definitive explanation about the origin of the name, and the very boundaries of the neighborhood are blurry and rather flexible. The double-triangle formed by Broadway, Columbus Avenue, 63rd Street, and 66th Street had been known, in the late 19th Century, as Empire Square or Empire Park. It wasn't until May, 1906, when the city’s Board of Aldermen passed a resolution, that the area was named “Lincoln Square". The Aldermen left no explanation of their decision and, according to researchers who've scoured past editions of The New York Times and of The Brooklyn Eagle, there is still no explanation for the name. The square itself is surrounded by the Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts which includes the Metropolitan Opera House, Avery Fisher Concert Hall, and the David Koch Theater for dance and ballet. Pay attention to the fountain in the middle which spouts water 30 feet into the air to create a mist that cools off anyone standing nearby.

  • Carnegie Hall
    •

    How do you get to Carnegie Hall? Practice, practice, practice! An appropriate joke for one of the most renowned concert halls in the world. In the spring of 1887, on board a ship traveling from New York to London, newlyweds Andrew Carnegie (the rich industrialist) and Louise Whitfield (daughter of a well-to-do New York merchant) were on their way to the groom’s native Scotland for their honeymoon. Also on board was the 25-year-old Walter Damrosch, who had just finished his second season as conductor and musical director of the Symphony Society of New York. Over the course of the voyage, the couple developed a friendship with Damrosch, inviting him to visit them in Scotland. It was there that Damrosch discussed his vision for a new concert hall in New York City. Carnegie expressed interest in committing a portion of his enormous wealth to the project, and the idea of Carnegie Hall was born. From this friendship grew a legendary concert hall whose allure has drawn the world’s greatest artists to its stages, setting the standard for excellence in music for more than a century. Gustav Mahler, Leopold Stokowski, Vladimir Horowitz, Maria Callas, Liza Minnelli, Paul Robeson, Bob Dylan all made their mark at Carnegie Hall. Andrew Carnegie proclaimed at the ceremonial laying of the cornerstone in 1890, “It is built to stand for ages, and during these ages it is probable that this Hall will intertwine itself with the history of our country.” Also, some of the most prominent political figures, authors, and intellectuals have appeared at Carnegie Hall, from Woodrow Wilson and Theodore Roosevelt to Mark Twain and Booker T. Washington.

  • Jackie Onasis Reservoir
    •

    The Jackie Kennedy Onassi (JKO) reservoir holds one BILLION gallons of water, but despite that, none of it is used to distribute New York City's water supply! Though the reservoir was originally used for that purpose when it was built in 1862, is has only been used to supply the pool and Harlem Meer since 1993 when it was decommissioned and renamed from the Central Park Reservoir to honor Jackie Kennedy who lived nearby on Fifth Avenue and would often jog around the reservoir, as many people still enjoy doing today. Today, if you make your way around the reservoir, you could see several different kinds of cherries being grown and upwards of twenty water birds, which make the JKO a popular place for New Yorkers and tourists alike.

  • Bethesda Terrace
    •

    Whether you have been to Bethesda Terrace in person or not, you have likely seen it before in a popular movie, TV show or video game, such as Enchanted, Grand Theft Auto IV, The Avengers, Elf, etc. Located on 72nd Street, it is considered the middle of the park and perfect for viewing The Lake, as well as connecting to the Bandshell and Central Park Malls.   • Panther Hill

  • Strawberry Fields
    •

    Strawberry Fields, named for the Beatles' song, "Strawberry Fields Forever," is a 2.5 acre section of Central Park located around 72nd Street and Central Park West. Despite being very large, it is best known for its iconic "Imagine" mosaic near the entrance to commemorate the life of John Lennon who was shot across the street. A gift from the city of Naples, the mosaic is often covered with flowers, candles and other memorial gifts. Depending on the time of year you visit, other musicians may also be memorialized in the park, such as Jerry Lewis and George Harrison, just keep in mind that the rest of the time Strawberry Fields is one of Central Park's designated quiet zones.

  • Obelisk
    •

    When Thutmose III of Upper and Lower Egypt died in the 1420’s BCE, he could have never imagined that one of the obelisks erected in his honor would end up thousands of miles away on what was then an oyster-lined garden island carefully tended by an unknown civilization. The monument was brought to New York on a surge of public interest in Egyptian antiquity in the late 19th century and erected in 1881. There is a nearly identical obelisk in London. Before this obelisk stood tall in our park, Cleopatra has it erected in Alexandria as part of a temple to honor Julius Ceasar.

  • Harlem Meer
    •

    Central Park’s northernmost water body used to be the most inland extremity of a tidal estuary that cut in from the East River. It lies in the shadow of the North Woods’ famous Blockhouse, a fortification that dates to Revolutionary times. Today, the Meer is a popular fishing spot. Despite its popularity and isolation from other bodies of water, the Meer is suffering from an introduction of the Asian Northern Snakehead, an invasive fish species that has been decimating northeastern freshwater ecosystems since 2002.

Lower Manhattan

  • The High Line
    •

    Currently the Highline is one mile long but is constantly under construction and looks to be one and a half miles long upon its completion this fall. Starting at Gansevoort Street it will eventually reach all the way to 34th street. Built on a former elevated railway, the Highline is all about originality in architecture and art.   • Bowling Green Park
    •

    Bowling Green is New York City’s oldest park. According to tradition, this spot served as the council ground for Native American tribes and was the site of the legendary sale of Manhattan to Peter Minuit in 1626. The Dutch called the area "the Plain" and used it for several purposes. It was the beginning of Heere Staat (High Street, now Broadway)—a trade route which extended north through Manhattan and the Bronx. It was also the site of a parade ground, meeting place, and cattle market. In 1686 the site became public property, when the City Charter put all "waste, vacant, unpatented and unappropriated lands" under municipal domain. Bowling Green was first designated as a park in 1733, when it was offered for rent at the cost of one peppercorn per year. Lessees John Chambers, Peter Bayard, and Peter Jay were responsible for improving the site with grass, trees, and a wood fence "for the Beauty and Ornament of the Said Street as well as for the Recreation and delight of the Inhabitants of this City." A gilded lead statue of King George III was erected here in 1770, and the iron fence (now a New York City landmark) was installed in 1771. On July 9, 1776, after the first public reading in New York State of the Declaration of Independence, this monument was toppled by angry citizens, dragged up Broadway, sent to Connecticut, melted down, and recast as ammunition. Portions of the statue are held by the Museum of the City of New York and the New-York Historical Society (which also possesses musket balls made from the statue’s lead).

    By the late 18th century, Bowling Green marked the center of New York’s most fashionable residential area, surrounded by rows of Federal-style townhouses. In 1819 the Common Council voted that neighbors could plant and tend the area in return for the exclusive use of the park by their families. By mid-century, shipping offices inhabited the old townhouses, and the park was returned to more public use. Monuments installed in the park in the 19th century include two fountains (now gone) and a statue of New York’s first mayor, Abraham De Peyster (1896, by George Bissell). In the first decade of the 20th century, Bowling Green was disrupted by the construction of the IRT subway. The park was rebuilt as part of citywide improvements made in preparation for visitors to the 1939 World’s Fair. Renovations to Bowling Green included removing the fountain basin, relocating the interior walkways, installing new benches, and providing new plantings. Mayor Fiorello LaGuardia, Parks Commissioner Robert Moses, members of the Board of Estimate, and local businessmen participated in the rededication ceremony, held April 6, 1939. Despite unseasonably late snow, the ceremony included a demonstration of colonial-era lawn bowling. A 1976-77 capital renovation restored Bowling Green to its 18th-century appearance. Improvements included the redistribution of subway entrances, the installation of new lampposts and benches, and landscaping. Publisher and philanthropist George Delacorte donated the park’s central fountain.

  • 60 Hudson Street
    •

    60 Hudson Street provides an important service for the world economy as the location of the computers and networks that traders use to access Wall Street. Known as the premier carrier hotel in the world, 60 Hudson Street houses over 300 different domestic and international networks. With a secure, power-protected environment to house and operate mission critical equipment, the building enables customers to achieve quick entry to their critical markets. In 2012 equipment for algorithmic trading was installed in the building in order to conduct trades microseconds more quickly than in Wall Street a mile away. The structure was built in 1928-30 as the headquarters of the Western Union Company, a purpose it served until 1973. The building contained offices, an auditorium, cafeteria and gymnasiums, classrooms so messengers could continue their education, shops and equipment rooms, along with 70 million feet of cable. The exterior brick moves from darker shades to lighter ones as the building rises, passing through 19 different colors as it does.

  • City Hall Park
    •

    You might notice how the ground slopes down just north of City Hall Park. This land was once part of Collect Pond, New York City’s only source of freshwater until the opening of the Croton Aqueduct in the 1840’s. By the early 19th century, the pond had grown fetid from industrial discharge and was hastily filled in around 1813. In precolonial times, the Canarsee village of Werpoes lined the pond’s southern side. Either by coincidence or some unrecorded interethnic understanding, the site of Werpoes would become the now-famous African Burial Ground. From the 1640s to the 1790s, ten of thousands of New York’s African-Americans were given traditional burials on the Collect Pond’s shores. The site was discovered in 1991 during the construction of a federal office building. Today, this bizarre corner of the city is the center of the NYC government, a political entity which governs a larger populace than that of Switzerland.

  • Brooklyn Bridge

    • The Brooklyn Bridge looms majestically over New York City’s East River, linking the two boroughs of Manhattan and Brooklyn. Since 1883, its granite towers and steel cables have offered a safe and scenic passage to millions of commuters and tourists, trains and bicycles, pushcarts and cars. The bridge’s construction took 14 years, involved 600 workers and cost $15 million (more than $320 million in today’s dollars). At least two dozen people died in the process, including its original designer. Now more than 125 years old, this iconic feature of the New York City skyline still carries roughly 150,000 vehicles and pedestrians every day. On May 24, 1883, the Brooklyn Bridge opened, connecting the then independent cities of New York and Brooklyn for the first time in history. Thousands of residents of Brooklyn and Manhattan Island turned out to witness the dedication ceremony, which was presided over by President Chester A. Arthur and New York Governor Grover Cleveland. Emily Roebling was given the first ride over the completed bridge, with a rooster, a symbol of victory, in her lap. With its unprecedented length and two stately towers, the Brooklyn Bridge was dubbed the “eighth wonder of the world.” For several years after its construction, it remained the tallest structure in the Western hemisphere. The connection it provided between the massive population centers of Brooklyn and Manhattan changed the course of New York City forever. In 1898, the city of Brooklyn formally merged with New York City, Staten Island and a few farm towns, forming Greater New York.

  • East River Park
    •

    The East River Park runs alongside the Franklin Delano Roosevelt (FDR) Drive and the East River from Montgomery Street to East 12th Street. It was conceived in the early 1930s when Robert Moses was designing the FDR (also known as East River) Drive. Moses knew that the expressway would pass through the Lower East Side, a neighborhood sorely in need of parkland. He was determined not to let the land between the expressway and the river go to waste. Moses envisioned a tree-shaded esplanade with abundant recreational facilities and windswept views of the East River and beyond.Moses soon faced a problem: the acquisition of enough land for a park in this densely populated area. Condemnation, the process by which the City may acquire private land for public purpose, was prohibitively expensive and fraught with legal difficulties, especially along this heavily industrialized waterfront. Moses arrived at an imaginative solution. To provide more parkland, he built a 10-foot wide concrete extension to Manhattan’s eastern shoreline spanning 20 blocks in length. The combination of the added platform and Moses’s energetic legal wrangling was enough to secure the needed land, and in 1939, East River Park -- the Lower East Side’s largest open space -- opened alongside the FDR Drive.

    The East River waterfront has played a crucial role in the development of New York City. Well before the Hudson River became a depot for the shipping industry, the East River provided a safe harbor for numerous shipping interests in the early days of colonial settlement. By 1825, the area was marked by an active shipbuilding industry, boisterous masses of sailors, and a number of active municipal waterfront markets. In the mid-19th century, as sea trade moved to the deeper channels of the Hudson River, docks gave way to factories, and then, in the late 19th century, to tenements. By the time Moses developed his plan for the park, the southern East River waterfront was dotted with slaughterhouses, glass factories, power stations, and railroad yards.

  • Seward Park
    •

    Over one hundred years ago, settlement workers Lillian D. Wald and Charles B. Stover founded the Outdoor Recreation League to promote organized games in public playgrounds as an alternative to play in city streets. Between 1898 and 1902 ORL opened nine privately sponsored playgrounds on municipal parkland. Soon after the City of New York assumed operations of ORL playgrounds in 1902, the facility at Seward Park became the first permanent, municipally built playground in the United States. It opened on October 17, 1903, in the north corner of the park. With its cinder surfacing, fences, recreation pavilion, and play and gymnastic equipment, the facility became a model for playground programming and design. The city had acquired the land for Seward Park by condemnation in 1897. Due to lack of funds, the site remained largely unimproved until the intervention of the ORL. In addition to the playground, the 1903 plan featured a large running track with an open play area in the center and a children’s farm garden in the southeast corner. Curving paths and a north-south mall divided the park into recreational areas. The limestone and terra cotta Seward Park Pavilion was equipped with marble baths, a gymnasium, and meeting rooms. Rocking chairs were placed on the broad porch for the use of mothers tending their small children.

  • Union Square Park

  • Madison Square Park
    •

    Madison Square Park is named for James Madison, 4th President of the United States and one of the principal authors of the constitution. However, before this became an illustrious New York location, the US Army ran drills and artillery exercises here and later housed juvenile delinquents on the site. In 1842, one of the first baseball teams - The New York Knickerbocker Base Ball Club - practiced where you now stand. When the park was being developed and finally opened in 1847, the team relocated to Hoboken.

    From your position, you should be able to spot two iconic buildings: The Met Life Tower and the New York Life Building. The Met Life Tower was built in 1909 and was the world’s tallest building for 3 years before it was surpassed in height by the Woolworth department store. The iconic skyscraper is now being repurposed as a hotel. The New York Life Insurance building is also notable with it’s gleaming, golden gilded spire despite it’s status as the 86th tallest building in NYC. (The tallest being the World Trade Center.) Completed in 1928, New York Life Insurance Company still calls this home.

  • Coralers Hook
    •

    In 1641, at a woodside tavern on the wilderness track linking New Amsterdam with the farming village of Haarlem (at approximately 2nd Avenue in the 40s or 50s), a disturbed young ethnic Wappinger carried out a revenge killing of the tavern’s owner, long-time colonist and friend of the local Indians, Claes Swits. This event rippled through the young colony and bent the recently arrived director, Willem Kieft, on a plan to drive Greater New York’s Algonquin population from the region. Going against the opinions of the colonial council, Kieft ordered attacks on Wappinger camps in Jersey City (pictured) and right here, at Corlear’s Hook. In one of the worst massacres in the history of the city, over a hundred twenty people, refugees driven south by marauding bands of Mohawk warriors, were murdered in cold blood by Kieft’s troops. This event resolved the feuding Algonquin bands to unify against the Dutch colony. The united tribes forced the entire European and African population to retreat to fortified town of New Amsterdam, which then didn’t extend north of Wall Street.

  • Jefferson Market Library
    •

    Originally a courthouse, the Jefferson Market Library has served the Greenwich Village community for over forty years. The building, a New York City landmark, was designed by architects Frederick Clark Withers and Calvert Vaux (who also assisted in the design of Central Park) in a Victorian Gothic style. It was erected (along with an adjacent prison and market) during the years 1875-1877 and cost the city almost $360,000. What the city got for its money, in addition to an architectural gem (it was voted one of the ten most beautiful buildings in America by a poll of architects in the 1880s) was a civil court (on the second floor, where the Adult Reading Room is now) and a police court (now the first-floor Children's Room). The beautiful brick-arched basement (now the Reference Room) was used as a holding area for prisoners on their way to jail or trial. By 1959, the building had become home only to pigeons and rats and was considered such an architectural eyesore the city planned to knock it down and erect an apartment building. But Village community members, led by Margot Gayle and Philip Wittenberg, and including Lewis Mumford, E.E. Cummings (who lived across the street in Patchin Place) and actor Maurice Evans, rallied to save the building from the wrecking ball. In 1961, Mayor Robert F. Wagner announced that it would be preserved and converted into a public library.

  • New Museum
    •

    The New Museum began as an idea in the mind of founding Director Marcia Tucker. As a curator at the Whitney Museum of American Art from 1967 through 1976, Tucker observed firsthand that new work by living artists was not easily assimilated into the conventional exhibition and collection structure of the traditional art museum.The care and attention that these venerable institutions lavished on older, established artists and artworks was not yet being extended to art being made in the present. Interested in bringing the scholarly practices of these older institutions to younger artists and their work, Tucker imagined an institution devoted to presenting, studying, and interpreting contemporary art. When Tucker officially founded the New Museum on January 1, 1977, it was the first museum devoted to contemporary art established in New York City since the Second World War. Positioned between a traditional museum and an alternative space, the New Museum’s stated mission was to be a catalyst for a broad dialogue between artists and the public by establishing “an exhibition, information, and documentation center for contemporary art made within a period of approximately ten years prior to the present.” The Museum presented the work of living artists who did not yet have wide public exposure or critical acceptance to a broader public.

  • Pier 15
    •

    Once the site of many a tavern complete with house of ill-repute nearby, the area where Fulton Street meets the East River was one of the largest commercial seaports in America from about 1820 - 1860. After the commercial traffic into the port slowed down, the neighborhood fell into disrepair and lay crumbling until the 1960s and 70s when private donors began buying the historic properties, renovating them, and repurposing them as attractions and historical landmarks. The ships you see moored nearby represent one of the largest private fleets in the country, and several of the boats were constructed before 1900. Can you guess which one is the oldest? It is The Wavertree, built in 1885 in England and carries the distinction of being the largest iron sailing vessel still afloat.

    If you have time, find the restaurant and tavern named The Bridge, which claims to be the oldest standing bar in Manhattan. Plus, just 2 doors down from it stands a building that once held contests to see how many rats a rat-terrier could kill in a single day.

  • Charging Bull Statue
    •

    After the stock market crash of 1987, a local New York artist Arturo Di Modica created the Bull sculpture. During the night of December 15, 1989, without authorization from city officials, Arturo Di Modica installed this sculpture in the middle of Broad Street, outside the New York Stock Exchange, beneath a 60 foot Christmas Tree. This was the one time that a bull was not welcome on Wall Street, and it was removed by the city the next day. Following a public outcry, the Bull was brought back into Manhattan, but moved to just north of Bowling Green Park (near 26 Broadway). This spot eventually became its permanent home. Today, visitors acquire luck from the bull by rubbing his horns, nose, and um...more delicate parts.

    The bull represents financial virility and when stocks as a whole are increasing in value, it is said to be a bull market. Conversely, the term used to describe a downward trend in the overall economy is a bear market. Most of the financial success in New York is generated by a FIRE economy (an acronym that stands for Finance, Insurance, and Real Estate).
